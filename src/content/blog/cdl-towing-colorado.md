## The short answer

Usually no CDL for a standard non-commercial tow — but verify against current Colorado rules for your weights and use, and make sure your truck is properly set up.

This is one of the most common questions we get, and the honest answer is: **for typical non-commercial use behind a standard pickup, generally no** — but weight and how you're using the trailer can change it. Here's how to think about it.

> **Heads up:** licensing rules change and depend on your exact setup. Treat this as a starting point, not legal advice — verify the current Colorado requirements for your specific weights and use before you tow.

## What usually doesn't require a CDL

Most homeowners and small contractors towing a single rental trailer behind a properly rated pickup, for their own non-commercial project, fall under the threshold and don't need a commercial license. A standard driver's license is typically enough for everyday trailer towing.

## What can change the answer

A few things push you toward heavier-license territory:

- **Combined weight.** As the gross combined weight rating of your truck and loaded trailer climbs, you can cross into classes that require a different license. Heavy equipment trailers and big loads are where this comes up.
- **Commercial use.** Hauling as part of a business, for hire, can change which rules apply versus moving your own materials.
- **Trailer weight on its own.** A heavy trailer over certain ratings can matter independently of the truck.

Because the exact numbers and combinations decide it, check the current Colorado DMV / DOT rules — or ask us about the specific trailer you're renting.

## What your truck actually needs

License aside, every loaded trailer needs the basics done right: the **correct hitch and ball**, a **brake controller** in the tow vehicle for heavier trailers, and a **tow rating** that covers the combined loaded weight. We confirm all of this at pickup so you leave legal and safe.

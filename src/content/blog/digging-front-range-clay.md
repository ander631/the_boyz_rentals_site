## The short answer

Front Range clay is heavy when wet, hard when dry, and slow when frozen — so don't undersize the machine. A 3 to 3.5 ton class handles most residential digs; go bigger for depth, volume, or rocky foothill ground.

If you're digging anywhere around Denver, you're almost certainly working in **expansive clay**. It's the default Front Range soil, and it behaves differently than the loamy ground people picture. Plan for it and your dig goes smoothly; ignore it and you'll fight the ground all day.

## What "expansive clay" means for your dig

Expansive clay **swells when it takes on moisture and shrinks when it dries out**. In practice that means:

- **Wet clay digs heavy and sticky** — it clings to the bucket and loads the machine.
- **Dry clay packs hard** — it can be like digging into pavement.
- **Early spring, it can still be frozen** — the top several inches hold winter long after the air warms up.

This is also why footings and drainage matter so much here: ground that moves with moisture is hard on anything you build on it.

## Sizing the machine to the soil

This is where renting the right size pays off:

- A **1-ton mini excavator** will struggle in frozen or heavy Front Range clay. It just doesn't have the weight for breakout force.
- A **3 to 3.5 ton mini excavator** has the heft to work dense clay and is the practical default for most residential digs here.
- For **deep work past 8–10 feet, or large volumes**, step up to a [full-size excavator](/excavator-rental/).

Near the **foothills** — west Arvada, Lakewood's Green Mountain, west Golden — the clay mixes with rock and decomposed granite, which is harder still. Lean toward more machine, not less.
